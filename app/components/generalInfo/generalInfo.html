Montserrat, known as the Emerald Isle of the Caribbean for its lush green rainforest and the Irish history ingrained in the ancestry of the island. Located in the Leeward Islands, part of the chain known as the Lesser Antilles in the British West Indies, it is a British Overseas Territory and has a UK-appointed governor.

You can travel to Montserrat via plane or ferry from neighbouring Antigua. The island is different from others in its unspoilt nature, it’s a place where you can experience the Caribbean as it’s truly lived. Crime is almost unknown in this peaceful, under populated country. Home to about 5,000 people, the sense of community is evident with values of courtesy and honesty displayed throughout.

The Volcano

Experience the full spectacle of natures wonder.

Montserrat is a volcanic island and forms part of the volcanic arc of the Lesser Antilles. The island was still trying to recover from the damage caused by Hurricane Hugo in 1989 when after 500 years of dormancy seismic activity started on Montserrat

In 1995, the Soufriere Hills Volcano on Montserrat erupted and over the next two years destroyed the island’s capital city of Plymouth, causing two thirds of the island to be declared an exclusion zone. Photos attest to the suddenness of the volcano’s eruption, which caused people to flee, leaving behind stores packed with merchandise and homes filled with belongings, now coated in more than 15 feet of volcanic ash.

Prior to the volcano there were over 12,000 people living on this island and it had an extremely active tourist and agriculture-based economy. The airport was about to be expanded and celebrity musicians were regular visitors to George Martin’s AIR studios, where the likes of Paul McCartney and Elton John recorded albums. George Martin’s house in the village of Salem has since been reborn as one of the island’s hotels, Olveston House.

Plymouth is now known as the modern day Pompeii, visitors can experience the eeriness and astounding wonder of natures triumph. Either viewed from a distance at the Montserrat Volcano Observatory or on foot with a guided tour into the exclusion zone, travellers can see the marvel of the volcano.

A scattering of department stores and government buildings still hold their heads in a landscape of ash and boulders. Vegetation has grown over homes and buildings which escaped the pyroclastic flows and in the lobby of a luxury hotel outside town, dusty papers still sit on the reception desk.

Montserrat is on the rebuild, the current hazard level on the mountainous Caribbean island is Level 1, which indicates more than one year without measured activity. New economy lies in extracting and exporting sand from the volcanic ash. Even more significant is the drilling of three geothermal wells, which should generate 200 years’ worth of power.

The History

Arrival of Irish settlers

Montserrat's Irish heritage dates back to the 17th century. The island became a haven for Irish Catholics who were affected by anti-Catholic legislations in Great Britain and Ireland. The predominantly Irish population of Montserrat is evident in the 1678 census which indicates that Seventy percent of Montserrat’s white population self-identified as ancestry can be identified today through the fact that the majority of the islanders have an Irish surname.

The plantation era

During the plantation era, colonists built an economy on sugar, rum, arrowroot and island cotton. Planters emerged from among the island’s Irish residents and the importation of African slaves began.

The rebellion

Soon slaves outnumbered the planters and started to rebel. In 1768, the slaves planned an island-wide attack on St. Patrick's Day, when the planters would be celebrating. Servants were instructed to grab all the weapons they could find inside the Government House while field slaves stormed the building with rocks, farm tools, clubs and homemade swords.

Despite months of plotting, their plans had been leaked and their revolt was unsuccessful resulting in the brutal killing of nine plot leaders.

St. Patrick's

In sustained honour of the island's rich culture and dynamic mix of Irish and African heritage, Montserrat commemorates St. Patrick's Day as a weeklong event celebrating their ancestors bravery and their fight for freedom. Festivities held that week commemorate the culture of Montserrat in song, dance, food and traditional costumes

The celebrations

Montserrat is the only country outside Ireland where St Patrick's Day is a public holiday.

The week-long St Patrick's festival provides a dynamic mix of Irish and African heritage, inclusive of traditional Caribbean entertainment, making it the most popular annual event.

Guinness and 'Green' Heineken are available in bars aside the customary rum punch cocktails.

Much of the activity on St Patrick's Day itself (March 17th) will centre around Festival Village, in the town of Salem. Here, stalls will sell traditional food such as salted codfish and sweet potato, “Dookna'' and play games such as “Zig Zag Zaggett” similar to marbles but played with cashew seeds.

Masquerade dancing is a focal aspect of the celebrations, masked dancers in costumes complete with bells, ribbons and attractive headdress resembling a bishop mitre, put on colourful displays of dance in a playful mockery of the one-time slave masters.

During St Patrick's Day, visitors will notice many locals wearing national dress - in which green is the dominant colour and both Guinness and 'Green' Heineken are available in bars alongside the customary rum punch cocktails.

African-inspired events such as the Freedom Run and masquerade dancing, commemorate the slave history in Montserrat, specifically the unsuccessful uprising that took place on St Patrick's Day in 1768.
